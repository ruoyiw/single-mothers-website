Why planning is important
Planning is very important to effectively advocate. Planning increases the chances of achieving the desired outcome. It increases the potential of the advocate to be calm, well-prepared, to not be ‘caught off guard’, and helps ensure any resources involved can be maximized.
The three main steps in the planning process include:
  1. Brainstorming possible actions.
  2. Evaluating the options.
      3. This might include:
    • gathering information e.g. about a school policy
    • researching one of the options so you can work out if it is a good way to go
  3. Planning the steps in detail.
List of skills that contribute to good planning
    Skills that underpin these tasks include the individual’s ability to:
  • Recognize all the facts influencing the task
  • Identify the key people that need to be involved
  • Establish stages or timeframes for when actions may take place and how and when to monitor progress
  • Think critically
  • Ask relevant questions
  • Summarise complex or difficult points or issues
  • Recognize the need for support networks at the beginning of the advocacy process.
How to find relevant legislation
    Finding relevant legislation is very important when advocating in some situations.
    Examples:
  • If you think you or your child is being discriminated against on the grounds of disability or race or gender etc
  • If you think a payday lender has cheated you, or a landlord is trying to take advantage of you.
    In order to find any relevant legislation, you can:
  • Contact responsible Government departments
  • Ask other service providers
  • Use the internet
  • Check in libraries (if possible law libraries)
How to find sources of information
  • Read a copy of the relevant ‘Act’ when visiting the responsible government or agency
  • Asking agencies or government departments if there is a user friendly guide for consumers to read
  • Visit a local Community legal Centre to see if they can or are running information sessions on this particular topic or refer to: http://www.lawhandbook.org.au/contents/

    This is a fantastic summary of areas of the law, which is a practical plain English guide to the law.

  • Download legislation from the internet (most Acts are large and it may be more cost effective to save a copy rather than print)
  • Approach your local member of Parliament and asking to read their copy of the ‘Act’
